## 1. Why Africa Is the Right Primary Market

The existing GTM document (40-C2-GTM-Strategy.md) identifies 150+ countries blocked from Palantir/Anduril due to ITAR/cost/sovereignty. Africa is the largest concentration of these countries and has specific structural factors that make it a viable early market:

1. **Active operational demand**: AU and ECOWAS run live peacekeeping operations (ATMIS in Somalia, MNJTF in the Lake Chad Basin, various ECOWAS missions in West Africa) that need real situational awareness and have operational budgets
2. **Low existing C2 capability**: Most forces currently use paper maps, WhatsApp, and VHF radio — the baseline is low, so MPE's modest capability is a genuine upgrade
3. **No ITAR restriction**: UK-developed software is not subject to US ITAR (though UK export control applies — see document 44)
4. **AU/ECOWAS procurement channels**: Structured, multilateral procurement that can reach many countries through one contract
5. **Chinese/Russian alternatives are losing ground**: Geopolitical shifts post-2022 have made AU/ECOWAS more open to Western-aligned (but not US-dependent) solutions

## 2. AU and ECOWAS Peacekeeping Structure

### African Union Peace Support Operations

The AU Peace and Security Council (PSC) authorises peace support operations. The AU maintains the **African Standby Force (ASF)** — a 25,000-strong rapid deployment force organised into five regional brigades:

| Brigade | Region | Lead Nation |
|---------|--------|-------------|
| EASF | East Africa Standby Force | Kenya (HQ Nairobi) |
| NASBRIG | North Africa | Egypt (HQ Cairo) |
| ECOBRIG | West Africa (ECOWAS) | Nigeria (HQ Abuja) |
| FOMAC | Central Africa | Cameroon |
| SADCBRIG | Southern Africa | South Africa (HQ Gaborone) |

Each brigade maintains a **Planning Element (PLANELM)** responsible for operational planning, doctrine, and C2 system requirements.

The AU Department of Peace and Security (DAPS) in Addis Ababa is the coordinating body. The **African Peace and Security Architecture (APSA)** is the overarching framework.

**Key AU contacts for C2 procurement:**
- AU Commission — Peace and Security Department, Addis Ababa
- AU Centre for Post-Conflict Reconstruction and Development (CPRD)
- African Centre for the Study and Research on Terrorism (ACSRT) — Algiers (counter-terrorism C2)

### ECOWAS Security Framework

ECOWAS has the most mature regional security structure. The 2025 **ECOWAS Protocol on Democracy and Security** (passed by ECOWAS Parliament, most significant reform since 1999 Mechanism) created:

- **ECOWAS Standby Integration Force (ESIF)**: Can be mobilised solely under ECOWAS authority (not requiring UN mandate)
- Pre-approved rules of engagement aligned with AU and UN frameworks
- Integrated intelligence-sharing between member states
- Rapid deployment capability for conflict prevention

**ECOWAS security institutions relevant to MPE:**
- **ECOWAS Early Warning and Response Network (ECOWARN)**: Based in Abuja — monitors conflict indicators across West Africa. An intelligence fusion tool — directly analogous to what MPE provides.
- **Inter-Governmental Action Group against Money Laundering in West Africa (GIABA)**: Less relevant
- **ECOWAS Standby Force Secretariat**: Manages the ESIF capability

**The ECOWARN use case is compelling**: ECOWARN currently collects reports from field monitors, compiles them manually, and distributes situation reports. MPE's pattern-of-life, alert engine, and SITREP generation could directly augment ECOWARN. This is the entry point.

## 3. How African Militaries Actually Procure C2 Systems

### Formal Procurement Rules

All African countries nominally follow formal procurement rules: tender boards for goods above a threshold value, competitive bidding encouraged, parliamentary oversight (in principle). The process mirrors other ministries.

**In practice**, defence procurement is opaque and non-transparent in most African countries:
- Ethiopia: military procurement rarely appears in the defence budget; hardware purchased without public visibility
- Nigeria: military has purchased equipment without parliamentary approval or Ministry of Defence knowledge
- Most countries: "confidentiality" justification prevents scrutiny

**What this means for MPE:** The formal procurement process exists but is not the primary access route. Relationship-based access to senior defence officials is equally important.

### Actual Procurement Channels

Based on research into how C2 and surveillance systems actually get procured in Africa:

**Channel 1: Bilateral Defence Agreements**
The most common route for foreign defence technology companies. Requires:
- Government-to-government (G2G) framework agreement between UK and target country
- UK government sponsor (DASA, MoD, FCDO trade mission)
- Ministry of Defence point of contact in the target country
- Demonstration to senior military/MoD officials

**Channel 2: Multilateral Peacekeeping Budgets**
AU missions are funded by:
- AU member state contributions (Peace Fund, operational from 2023)
- UN assessed contributions (for UN-mandated missions)
- EU APSA support (European Union covers ~75% of AU peacekeeping costs via the African Peace Facility)
- US Africa Command (AFRICOM) security cooperation funding
- ECOWAS own-source revenue

For MPE, the **EU Africa Peace Facility (APF)** is significant: it specifically funds equipment and training for AU peace support operations. Software tools that improve AU operational effectiveness qualify.

**Channel 3: Defence Industry Exhibitions**
- **DSEI** (London, biennial — next 2025): The main international defence exhibition. AU/ECOWAS procurement officials attend.
- **MSPO** (Poland): NATO focus, less Africa relevance
- **Africa Aerospace and Defence (AAD)** (South Africa, biennial): The main African defence show. SANDF, Nigerian Army, Kenyan Defence Forces, AU representatives all attend. Critical for African market entry.
- **Milipol** (Paris, biennial): Security/police focus — relevant for gendarmerie/police applications

**Channel 4: UN Mission Procurement**
UN DPPA (Department of Political and Peacebuilding Affairs) and DPKO (now DPO) procure C2 tools through the UN procurement system (Global Marketplace). UN missions (UNMISS, MINUSCA, MONUSCO) have their own C2 requirements and budgets.

### Country-Specific Procurement Intelligence

**Nigeria** (largest African defence budget, ~$5.5B):
- Defence procurement through the Nigerian Defence Industries Corporation (DICON) and MoD
- Significant air surveillance and maritime domain awareness gaps
- Nigerian Navy requires maritime C2 (piracy in Gulf of Guinea)
- Nigerian Air Force operates drones (CH-3A, Bayraktar TB2 ordered) — needs C2 integration
- Entry route: Lagos-based defence consultancy + MoD introduction

**Ghana** (anglophone West Africa, stable democracy):
- Ghana Armed Forces (GAF) are well-regarded regionally
- Participate heavily in ECOWAS and UN peacekeeping
- Less opaque procurement than Nigeria
- Entry route: High Commission London → GAF Directorate of Acquisition

**Kenya** (Eastern Africa hub, EASF HQ):
- Kenya Defence Forces (KDF) are the most technologically advanced in East Africa
- Active in ATMIS (Somalia), EASF leadership
- Strong interest in C-UAS given drone threats in Somalia border region
- EASF PLANELM in Nairobi is key contact for regional C2 requirements
- Entry route: British Army Training Unit Kenya (BATUK) connections, DSTI Kenya

**Ethiopia** (AU HQ location, complex politics):
- HQ of African Union = access to all AU institutions
- Complex post-Tigray conflict period — procurement is sensitive
- Ethiopian National Defence Force (ENDF) has significant C2 gaps
- Better approached via AU institutions than bilateral

**South Africa** (SADCBRIG, industrial base):
- Armscor is the procurement agency
- South Africa has its own defence industry (Denel) — will want local manufacture/content
- SADCBRIG participation makes SA a potential regional champion
- Most transparent procurement process on the continent

## 4. Specific Entry Points for MPE

### Near-Term (2026–2027): The Demo Path

**Target 1: ECOWARN Augmentation**
- Organisation: ECOWAS Early Warning Directorate, Abuja
- Use case: Replace/augment manual conflict monitoring with MPE's entity tracking + alert engine
- Why it works: MPE's intelligence.py (SITREP narration), pattern_of_life.py (anomaly detection), and alerts.py map directly to ECOWARN's mission
- How to reach them: ECOWAS HQ via UK trade mission or direct academic/research collaboration

**Target 2: AU Mission Planning**
- Organisation: AU PSOD (Peace Support Operations Division), Addis Ababa
- Use case: Common operating picture for ATMIS (Somalia) or MNJTF (Lake Chad)
- Why it works: Active operations with real C2 needs; EU Africa Peace Facility funding available
- How to reach them: Via DASA Africa programme or UK FCDO conflict stability team

**Target 3: Nigerian Navy Maritime Domain Awareness**
- Organisation: Nigerian Navy HQ (Apapa, Lagos)
- Use case: Gulf of Guinea piracy tracking — AIS monitoring + anomaly detection
- Why it works: MPE's AIS receiver + classifier + geofencing is a direct fit for maritime surveillance
- Current capability gap: Nigerian Navy does not have an automated AIS monitoring system
- How to reach them: Nigeria High Commission London → Naval Attaché

**Target 4: Kenya Defence Forces C-UAS**
- Organisation: KDF Directorate of Acquisition, Nairobi
- Use case: Counter-drone around Somalia border, Nairobi critical infrastructure
- Why it works: MPE's geofencing + alert engine for airspace violations; can integrate with RF sensors
- How to reach them: BATUK (British Army Training Unit Kenya) → KDF liaison

### Medium-Term (2027–2028): Multilateral Contracts

Once one AU/ECOWAS demonstration is successful:
- **APF (Africa Peace Facility) grant application** through EU-AU cooperation framework
- **ECOWAS bloc contract**: If ECOWAS adopts MPE for ECOWARN, it propagates to all 15 member states
- **African Peace Fund**: Operational from 2023, specifically funds AU PSO capabilities

## 5. Pricing and Commercial Model for Africa

African defence budgets are real but limited. Palantir's $10M+ contracts are inaccessible. The right model:

**SaaS hosted model (lowest barrier):**
- MPE hosted on cloud (AWS/Azure) in EU or Africa region
- ATAK-CIV (free) on Android tablets as client
- Monthly subscription per mission/deployment: $5,000–15,000/month
- Includes: engine, TAK Server, storage, operator API
- 12-month mission contract = $60,000–180,000

**Deployed model (for sovereignty-sensitive customers):**
- MPE on a hardened mini-PC (Intel NUC or similar) deployed on-site
- Annual software licence + support: $30,000–80,000
- Hardware sold separately or sourced locally
- Sovereignty: customer owns hardware, MPE provides software updates

**Pilot programme:**
- 3-month free pilot with one AU/ECOWAS mission
- One MPE developer embedded with the mission for first 30 days
- Outcome: validated capability demonstration → procurement case to donor/funder

## 6. Key Risks

| Risk | Probability | Mitigation |
|------|------------|-----------|
| Export licence delays | High | See document 44; apply for SIEL early, use OGEL where possible |
| Procurement opacity | High | Build relationships at senior level, not just formal tender process |
| Connectivity in operational areas | Medium | MPE must work on low-bandwidth/intermittent links; optimise CoT packet size |
| Chinese C2 alternatives undercutting on price | Medium | Compete on sovereignty (UK vs China), interoperability (CoT/ATAK), support quality |
| Coup/political instability in customer country | Medium | Diversify across multiple countries; avoid single-customer concentration |
| Denel/SA local content requirements | Low-Medium | Partnership with SA tech company for local manufacturing requirement |
